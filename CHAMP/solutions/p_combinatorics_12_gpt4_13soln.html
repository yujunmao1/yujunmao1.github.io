Along a one-way street there are n parking lots. One-by-one n cars numbered 1 to n enter the street. Each driver i heads to his favorite parking lot a_i, and, if it is free, he occupies it. Otherwise, he continues to the next free lot and occupies it. But if all succeeding lots are occupied, he leaves for good. How many sequences {a_1, ..., a_n} are such that every driver can park, as an expression of n?

Below is a partial solution to the problem that may be helpful:

1. Make the parking lot arrangement a circle, by connecting the n-th lot to a new (n+1)-th lot, which connects back to the 1st lot.
2. Thus, every car will have somewhere to park because there are n cars in the (n+1)-lot circle.
The problem can be solved using the concept of derangements in combinatorics. 

A derangement is a permutation of the elements of a set, such that no element appears in its original position. In this case, a derangement would mean a situation where no driver gets to park in his favorite parking lot.

Let's denote the number of derangements of n elements as D(n). It is known that D(n) = n!(1 - 1/1! + 1/2! - 1/3! + ... + (-1)^n/n!).

Now, let's consider the total number of ways to assign favorite parking lots to drivers. Since there are n drivers and n parking lots, this is simply n!.

However, we want to exclude the cases where at least one driver gets to park in his favorite parking lot. These are exactly the derangements, so we subtract D(n) from n!.

Therefore, the number of sequences {a_1, ..., a_n} such that every driver can park is n! - D(n).

But, we have to consider the circular arrangement of parking lots. In this case, each sequence can be rotated in n different ways, and each rotation gives a valid sequence. Therefore, we have to multiply the result by n.

So, the final answer is n(n! - D(n)).

Note: This is a general expression. For specific values of n, you would need to calculate D(n) and substitute it into the expression.
