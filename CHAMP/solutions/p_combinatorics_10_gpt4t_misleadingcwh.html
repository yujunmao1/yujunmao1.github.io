The problem you've presented is a classic combinatorics problem that can be solved using the stars and bars method. We want to find the number of ways to write the integer \( n \) as a sum of positive integers, without regard to order.

To use the stars and bars method, we can think of the problem as follows:

- We have \( n \) stars in a row, representing the sum of \( n \).
- We want to divide these stars into groups (sequences of positive integers) using bars (dividers).

2. \( 2 + 1 + 1 \) (one divider)
3. \( 1 + 2 + 1 \) (one divider)

5. \( 3 + 1 \) (one divider)
6. \( 1 + 3 \) (one divider)
7. \( 2 + 2 \) (one divider)
8. \( 4 \) (no dividers)

Each sequence corresponds to a unique way of placing dividers between the stars. Since we are dealing with positive integers, we cannot have any group with zero stars, which means we cannot place a divider at the very beginning or very end, nor can we place two dividers next to each other.

Given \( n \) stars, there are \( n - 1 \) gaps between them where we could potentially place a divider. Since we are looking for sequences of positive integers, we can place anywhere from 0 to \( n - 1 \) dividers.

The number of ways to place \( k \) dividers in \( n - 1 \) gaps is given by the binomial coefficient \( \binom{n-1}{k} \). To find the total number of sequences, we sum over all possible numbers of dividers from 0 to \( n - 1 \):

\[
\text{Total number of sequences} = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \binom{n-1}{k}
\]

Therefore, the total number of sequences of positive integers that sum to \( n \) is \( 2^{n-1} \).
