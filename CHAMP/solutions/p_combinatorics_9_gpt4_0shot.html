Consider all 2^n−1 nonempty subsets of the set {1, 2,..., n}. For every such subset, we find the product of the reciprocals of each of its elements. Find the sum of all these products, as an expression of n.
Let's denote the set {1, 2,..., n} as S. We want to find the sum of the products of the reciprocals of all nonempty subsets of S. 

Let's consider a single element from S, say k. There are 2^(n-1) subsets of S that contain k (since for each of the other n-1 elements, we can choose to either include it or not, giving us 2^(n-1) possibilities). 

In each of these subsets, the reciprocal of k contributes a factor of 1/k to the product. Therefore, the total contribution from all subsets containing k is 2^(n-1) * 1/k.

Since this is true for all k in S, we can sum over all k from 1 to n to get the total sum of the products of the reciprocals of all nonempty subsets of S:

Sum = 2^(n-1) * (1/1 + 1/2 + 1/3 + ... + 1/n)

This is the harmonic series, and its nth partial sum is approximately ln(n) (natural logarithm of n). Therefore, the sum of the products of the reciprocals of all nonempty subsets of S is approximately:

Sum ≈ 2^(n-1) * ln(n)

This is an approximation because the harmonic series diverges, and its nth partial sum is actually ln(n) + γ, where γ is the Euler-Mascheroni constant. However, for large n, the contribution from γ becomes negligible, so the approximation is quite accurate.
