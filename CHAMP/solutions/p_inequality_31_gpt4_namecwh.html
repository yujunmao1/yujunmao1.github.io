The inequality of arithmetic and harmonic means for n variables is a fundamental concept in mathematical analysis and number theory. It states that for any set of positive real numbers, the arithmetic mean is always greater than or equal to the harmonic mean. 

Let's define the two means first:

1. Arithmetic Mean (AM): The arithmetic mean of n positive real numbers (a1, a2, ..., an) is the sum of the numbers divided by the count of the numbers. 

   AM = (a1 + a2 + ... + an) / n

2. Harmonic Mean (HM): The harmonic mean of n positive real numbers (a1, a2, ..., an) is the reciprocal of the arithmetic mean of the reciprocals of the numbers.

   HM = n / [(1/a1) + (1/a2) + ... + (1/an)]

The inequality of arithmetic and harmonic means states that:

AM >= HM

This inequality holds true for all positive real numbers. If all the numbers in the set are equal, then the arithmetic mean equals the harmonic mean. Otherwise, the arithmetic mean is strictly greater than the harmonic mean.
