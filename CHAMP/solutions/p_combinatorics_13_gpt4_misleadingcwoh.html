Of 3n+1 objects, n are indistinguishable, and the remaining ones are distinct. In how many ways can we choose n objects, as an expression of n?

You may find the following information useful:

1. If there are n steps of doing a task, and there are p_i ways to do the i-th step, then there are p_1*p_2*...*p_n ways to do the task.
2. If we count each object d times and get a total of n counts, then there are n/d objects.
3. C(n, k)=n/k*C(n-1, k-1).
The problem can be solved by considering two cases:

Case 1: We choose all n objects from the indistinguishable ones. There is only 1 way to do this, since all the objects are indistinguishable.

Case 2: We choose some objects from the indistinguishable ones and the rest from the distinct ones. Let's say we choose k objects from the indistinguishable ones and (n-k) from the distinct ones. The number of ways to do this is C(n, k)*C(2n+1-n, n-k) = C(n, k)*C(n+1, n-k).

The total number of ways to choose n objects is the sum of the number of ways in each case. Therefore, the total number of ways is:

1 + Σ [C(n, k)*C(n+1, n-k)] for k=0 to n

This is the final answer.
