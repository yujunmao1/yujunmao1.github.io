The problem is asking for the maximum number of integer-valued zeros a polynomial of degree n can have if f(0) and f(1) are both odd.

First, let's consider the polynomial f(x) = a(x-x_1)(x-x_2)...(x-x_n). 

If f(0) is odd, then a*x_1*x_2*...*x_n is odd. Since the product of integers is odd if and only if all the integers are odd, this means that a and all the x_i's must be odd.

We have a contradiction here: the x_i's must be both odd and even. The only integer that is both odd and even is 0. So, all the x_i's must be 0.

Therefore, the polynomial can have at most one integer-valued zero, which is 0.
