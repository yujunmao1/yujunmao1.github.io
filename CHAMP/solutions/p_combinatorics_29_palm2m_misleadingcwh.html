A diagnoal in a convex polygon is any line connecting two non-adjacent vertices. For a convex polygon of n vertices, we draw all possible diagonals and suppose that no three diagonals pass through the same point. How many such intersection points do we have in the interior of the polygon, as an expression of n?

You may find the following information useful:

1. An intersection point is created from two diagnonals.
2. C(n, r)=n!/(r!*(n-r)!)=n*(n-1)*(n-2)*...*(n-r+1)/r!.
Let $n$ be the number of vertices in the polygon. The number of diagonals is $\binom{n}{2}$. For each diagonal, there are $n-2$ other diagonals that it can intersect. Therefore, the total number of intersection points is $\binom{n}{2}(n-2)$.

However, this includes intersection points that are on the boundary of the polygon. To count only the intersection points in the interior of the polygon, we need to subtract the number of intersection points on the boundary. The number of intersection points on the boundary is the number of ways to choose two non-adjacent vertices and connect them with a diagonal. This is $\binom{n}{2}-n$.

Therefore, the total number of intersection points in the interior of the polygon is $\binom{n}{2}(n-2)-\binom{n}{2}+n=\boxed{n(n-3)}$.
