Of 3n+1 objects, n are indistinguishable, and the remaining ones are distinct. In how many ways can we choose n objects, as an expression of n?

You may find the following information useful:

1. Find the number of ways to choose a collection containing k distinguishable objects.
The problem can be solved using the concept of combinations in combinatorics.

First, let's break down the problem:

We have 3n+1 objects, out of which n are indistinguishable and the remaining (3n+1 - n) = 2n+1 are distinct.

We need to find the number of ways to choose n objects out of these.

There are two cases to consider:

1. We choose some or all of the indistinguishable objects.
2. We do not choose any of the indistinguishable objects.

Case 1: We choose some or all of the indistinguishable objects

Since the indistinguishable objects are identical, we don't have to consider them separately. We just need to decide how many we take, which can be any number from 1 to n. For each choice, we need to fill up the rest of our selection with distinguishable objects.

If we choose k indistinguishable objects, we need to choose (n - k) distinguishable ones. The number of ways to do this is given by the binomial coefficient C(2n+1, n-k), where C(n, k) denotes the number of ways to choose k objects from n.

Summing over all possible values of k gives us the total number of ways for case 1:

Sum_{k=0}^{n} C(2n+1, n-k)

Case 2: We do not choose any of the indistinguishable objects

In this case, we need to choose all n objects from the 2n+1 distinguishable ones. The number of ways to do this is C(2n+1, n).

Adding the results from the two cases gives the total number of ways to choose n objects:

Sum_{k=0}^{n} C(2n+1, n-k) + C(2n+1, n)

This is the final answer.
