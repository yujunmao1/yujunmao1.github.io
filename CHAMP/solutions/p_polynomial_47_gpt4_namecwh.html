The binomial theorem describes the algebraic expansion of powers of a binomial. A binomial is a polynomial equation with two terms, typically written in the form (a + b). 

According to the binomial theorem, the expansion of (a + b)^n, where n is a non-negative integer, is given by:

(a + b)^n = Σ (from k=0 to n) [nCk * a^(n-k) * b^k]

where:
- nCk (read "n choose k") is a binomial coefficient that can be calculated as n! / [k!(n-k)!], where "!" denotes factorial,
- a^(n-k) and b^k are the terms of the binomial raised to the appropriate powers,
- Σ (from k=0 to n) indicates the sum of the terms generated by letting k range from 0 to n.

For example, if we want to expand (a + b)^3 using the binomial theorem, we get:

(a + b)^3 = 3C0*a^3*b^0 + 3C1*a^2*b^1 + 3C2*a^1*b^2 + 3C3*a^0*b^3
           = a^3 + 3a^2*b + 3a*b^2 + b^3

The binomial theorem is a fundamental theorem in algebra and is widely used in algebraic operations, calculus, and mathematical analysis.
