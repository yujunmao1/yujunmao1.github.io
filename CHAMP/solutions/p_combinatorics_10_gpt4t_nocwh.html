How many sequences of positive integer numbers have sum n, as an expression of n?

You may find the following information useful:

1. Consider a row of n points, with n-1 gaps.
2. Insert dividers into the gaps, and consider the correspondance between a sequence with a divider insertion configuration.
To solve this problem, we can use a combinatorial approach known as "stars and bars." The idea is to represent the sequence of positive integers as a sequence of stars, with bars (or dividers) used to separate different numbers in the sequence.

For example, if n = 5, one possible sequence is 1 + 1 + 1 + 1 + 1, which can be represented as:
```
* | * | * | * | *
```
Another sequence could be 2 + 3, represented as:
```
* * | * * *
```
The key observation is that every sequence of positive integers summing to n corresponds to a unique way of placing n - 1 dividers among the n - 1 gaps between n stars.

However, since we are dealing with positive integers, we cannot have any number in the sequence be zero. This means that we cannot place a divider at the very beginning or very end, and we cannot place two dividers directly next to each other.

To account for this, we can think of initially placing one star in each "slot" that will be created by the dividers, ensuring that each number in the sequence is at least 1. This leaves us with n - (number of dividers) stars to freely distribute.

Since we want the sum to be n, and we have n - 1 gaps, we need to place dividers in such a way that we create a sequence of positive integers. The number of dividers we can use is from 0 to n - 1 (since we can have a sequence of just one number, n, or n numbers, all being 1).

Now, we have n - 1 gaps and we want to choose some of them (possibly none, possibly all) to place our dividers. This is a classic combinatorial problem of choosing k items from a set of n items, which is given by the binomial coefficient "n choose k" or C(n, k).

In our case, we have n - 1 gaps and we can choose any number of dividers from 0 to n - 1 to place in these gaps. This means we need to sum over all possible numbers of dividers:

Sum from k = 0 to n - 1 of C(n - 1, k)

This is actually the sum of all the terms in the (n - 1)th row of Pascal's triangle, which is known to be 2^(n - 1).

Therefore, the number of sequences of positive integer numbers that have a sum of n is 2^(n - 1).
