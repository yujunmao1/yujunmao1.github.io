Given a binomial expression of the form (a + b), the Binomial Theorem provides a formula to expand this expression when raised to any positive integer exponent n.

The Binomial Theorem states that:

(a + b)ⁿ = Σ (from k=0 to n) [nCk * a^(n-k) * b^k]

where:
- n is a non-negative integer and is the power to which the binomial is raised.
- Σ denotes the summation symbol, indicating that you sum all the terms from k=0 to n.
- nCk (read as "n choose k") are the binomial coefficients, which can be calculated using the formula nCk = n! / [k! * (n-k)!], where "!" denotes factorial. The factorial of a non-negative integer x, denoted by x!, is the product of all positive integers less than or equal to x.
- a^(n-k) and b^k are the terms involving the variables a and b, with their exponents adding up to n for each term.

The binomial coefficients can also be found in Pascal's Triangle, where each number is the sum of the two numbers directly above it in the previous row.

The Binomial Theorem allows us to write out the expanded form of (a + b)ⁿ without having to multiply the binomial by itself n times. The theorem is particularly useful for large values of n, where manual expansion would be impractical.

For example, using the Binomial Theorem, the expansion of (a + b)³ would be:
