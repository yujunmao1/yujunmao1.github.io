Polynomial division is a process similar to long division for numbers, but it is used to divide one polynomial by another. The goal of polynomial division is to determine how many times the divisor polynomial can "fit" into the dividend polynomial, and to find the remainder if the division is not exact.

Here's the definition and a brief explanation of the terms involved:

**Dividend**: The polynomial that is being divided. This is analogous to the number you start with in numerical long division.

**Divisor**: The polynomial by which you are dividing the dividend. In numerical division, this would be the number you divide by.

**Quotient**: The result of the division, representing how many times the divisor can fit into the dividend without considering the remainder.

**Remainder**: The part of the dividend that is left over after the division process has been completed. If the degree of the remainder is less than the degree of the divisor, the division process is complete.

The process of polynomial division can be done using long division or synthetic division (which is a shortcut method applicable when the divisor is a binomial of the form x - c).

Here's a step-by-step outline of the long division process for polynomials:

1. Arrange both the dividend and the divisor polynomials in descending order of their degrees (the highest power of x comes first).

2. Divide the leading term (the term with the highest power of x) of the dividend by the leading term of the divisor. This gives you the first term of the quotient.

3. Multiply the entire divisor by the term you just found and subtract the result from the dividend. This gives you a new polynomial.

4. Repeat the process using the new polynomial as the dividend. Divide the leading term of the new polynomial by the leading term of the divisor to find the next term of the quotient.

5. Continue this process until the degree of the remainder polynomial is less than the degree of the divisor or until the remainder is zero.

6. The final quotient and remainder give you the result of the division, which can be expressed as:

Fermat's Little Theorem is a fundamental result in number theory, named after the 17th-century mathematician Pierre de Fermat. \[ a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \ (\text{mod} \ p) \]

\[ a^p \equiv a \ (\text{mod} \ p) \]

This second formulation includes the case where \( a \) is divisible by \( p \), as \( a \) modulo \( p \) would be 0, and \( a^p \) would also be divisible by \( p \), hence also congruent to 0 modulo \( p \).

Fermat's Little Theorem is often used to reduce large exponents in modular arithmetic calculations, as it provides a way to work with exponents that are multiples of \( p-1 \) by reducing them modulo \( p-1 \). This is particularly useful in computations involving large prime numbers.
