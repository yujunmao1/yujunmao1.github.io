The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality is a fundamental result in linear algebra and analysis, with numerous applications in various fields of mathematics and the sciences. It provides an upper bound for the absolute value of the inner product of two vectors in terms of the product of their norms.

Let's consider a real or complex inner product space, which is a vector space equipped with an inner product. The inner product of two vectors \( \mathbf{u} \) and \( \mathbf{v} \) is denoted by \( \langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle \). The norm of a vector \( \mathbf{u} \), denoted by \( \|\mathbf{u}\| \), is defined as the square root of the inner product of the vector with itself, i.e., \( \|\mathbf{u}\| = \sqrt{\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{u} \rangle} \).

The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality states that for any vectors \( \mathbf{u} \) and \( \mathbf{v} \) in an inner product space, the following inequality holds:

\[ |\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\| \cdot \|\mathbf{v}\| \]

In the context of Euclidean space \( \mathbb{R}^n \) with the standard dot product as the inner product, the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality can be written as:

\[ |\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|_2 \cdot \|\mathbf{v}\|_2 \]

where \( \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} \) is the dot product of \( \mathbf{u} \) and \( \mathbf{v} \), and \( \|\mathbf{u}\|_2 \) and \( \|\mathbf{v}\|_2 \) are the Euclidean (L2) norms of \( \mathbf{u} \) and \( \mathbf{v} \), respectively.

The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality has several important consequences, such as the triangle inequality for norms and the fact that the angle between two vectors in an inner product space is well-defined. It also plays a crucial role in proving other mathematical results and is widely used in optimization, probability theory, and quantum mechanics, among other areas.
