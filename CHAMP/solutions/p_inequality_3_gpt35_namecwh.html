Chebyshev's inequality is a fundamental result in probability theory that provides an upper bound on the probability that a random variable deviates from its mean by a certain amount. It is named after the Russian mathematician Pafnuty Chebyshev.

The inequality states that for any random variable X with a finite mean μ and variance σ^2, the probability that X deviates from its mean by more than k standard deviations is at most 1/k^2, where k is any positive number greater than 1.

Mathematically, Chebyshev's inequality can be expressed as:

P(|X - μ| ≥ kσ) ≤ 1/k^2

Here, |X - μ| represents the absolute deviation of X from its mean, kσ represents the distance from the mean in terms of standard deviations, and P(|X - μ| ≥ kσ) represents the probability that X deviates from its mean by more than k standard deviations.

Chebyshev's inequality is a powerful tool because it applies to any distribution, regardless of its shape. It provides a general bound on the probability of extreme events, even when the distribution is not known or is difficult to analyze.

Chebyshev's inequality is particularly useful when dealing with large datasets or when the distribution is not well-behaved.
