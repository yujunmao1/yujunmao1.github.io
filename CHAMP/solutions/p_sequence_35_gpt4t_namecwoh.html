The inequality of arithmetic and geometric means (AM-GM inequality) is a fundamental result in algebra that relates the arithmetic mean and the geometric mean of a set of non-negative real numbers. For any set of \( n \) non-negative real numbers \( a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n \), the AM-GM inequality states that the arithmetic mean is always greater than or equal to the geometric mean. That is:

\[
\frac{a_1 + a_2 + \cdots + a_n}{n} \geq \sqrt[n]{a_1 \cdot a_2 \cdot \ldots \cdot a_n}
\]

with equality if and only if \( a_1 = a_2 = \cdots = a_n \).

Here's a breakdown of the two means:

1. **Arithmetic Mean (AM)**: This is the sum of the numbers divided by the count of the numbers. For \( n \) numbers \( a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n \), the arithmetic mean is:

\[
AM = \frac{a_1 + a_2 + \cdots + a_n}{n}
\]

2. **Geometric Mean (GM)**: This is the \( n \)-th root of the product of the numbers. For \( n \) numbers \( a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n \), the geometric mean is:

\[
GM = \sqrt[n]{a_1 \cdot a_2 \cdot \ldots \cdot a_n}
\]

The AM-GM inequality is particularly useful because it gives a way to compare the averages of a set of numbers in a manner that is independent of the distribution of values within the set. It is widely used in various fields of mathematics, including optimization, number theory, and algebraic geometry.

The inequality can be proven using various methods, including induction, the power-mean inequality, or by using the concept of convexity and Jensen's inequality.
