Please explain the following concept: modular arithmetic rules.
Modular arithmetic, often referred to as "clock arithmetic," is a system of arithmetic for integers, where numbers "wrap around" after they reach a certain value—the modulus. The modern approach to modular arithmetic was developed by Carl Friedrich Gauss in his book "Disquisitiones Arithmeticae," published in 1801.

Here are some basic rules and properties of modular arithmetic:

1. Congruence Modulo n:
Two integers a and b are said to be congruent modulo n if their difference (a - b) is an integer multiple of n. This is denoted as:

a ≡ b (mod n)

This means that when a and b are divided by n, they leave the same remainder.

2. Addition:
If a ≡ b (mod n) and c ≡ d (mod n), then (a + c) ≡ (b + d) (mod n). In other words, the sum of two congruent numbers is also congruent to the sum of their respective equivalents.

3. Subtraction:
If a ≡ b (mod n) and c ≡ d (mod n), then (a - c) ≡ (b - d) (mod n). Similar to addition, the difference between two congruent numbers is congruent to the difference of their respective equivalents.

4. Multiplication:
If a ≡ b (mod n) and c ≡ d (mod n), then (a * c) ≡ (b * d) (mod n). The product of two congruent numbers is congruent to the product of their respective equivalents.

5. Division:
Division is a bit more complex in modular arithmetic. If a ≡ b (mod n) and c ≡ d (mod n), it is not necessarily true that a/c ≡ b/d (mod n). Division is only valid if the divisor is coprime with the modulus (i.e., the divisor and the modulus share no common factors other than 1). In such cases, you can multiply by the multiplicative inverse of the divisor modulo n to "divide."

6. Powers:
If a ≡ b (mod n), then for any positive integer k, a^k ≡ b^k (mod n). The power of a congruent number is congruent to the power of its equivalent.

7. Modular Inverses:
An integer a has a modular multiplicative inverse modulo n if there exists an integer x such that (a * x) ≡ 1 (mod n). Not every number has a modular inverse; a number a has an inverse modulo n if and only if a and n are coprime.

These rules make modular arithmetic a powerful tool in various fields of mathematics and computer science, especially in number theory, cryptography, and for performing computations with large numbers.
