The Monotone Convergence Theorem (MCT) is a fundamental result in real analysis, particularly in the context of measure theory and integration. It provides conditions under which the limit of a sequence of functions can be integrated by integrating the functions in the sequence. Here is the statement of the Monotone Convergence Theorem for non-decreasing sequences of functions:

Let \((f_n)_{n=1}^{\infty}\) be a sequence of non-negative measurable functions defined on a measure space \((X, \mathcal{M}, \mu)\), such that \(f_n(x) \leq f_{n+1}(x)\) for all \(x \in X\) and all \(n \in \mathbb{N}\). If \(f_n(x) \to f(x)\) pointwise as \(n \to \infty\) for almost every \(x \in X\), where \(f\) is also a measurable function, then
\[
\lim_{n \to \infty} \int_X f_n \, d\mu = \int_X f \, d\mu.
\]

In other words, if you have a sequence of non-negative functions that increase pointwise to some limit function \(f\), then the integral of the limit function \(f\) is the limit of the integrals of the functions in the sequence.

The Monotone Convergence Theorem is particularly useful because it allows us to interchange the limit and the integral, which is not always possible in more general settings. This theorem is a key tool in proving other important results in analysis, such as the Dominated Convergence Theorem and the Fatou's Lemma.

It's important to note that the Monotone Convergence Theorem requires the functions to be non-negative. This condition ensures that the integrals of the functions in the sequence are well-defined (possibly infinite) and that the sequence of integrals is non-decreasing, which is crucial for the conclusion of the theorem to hold.

The theorem can also be stated for non-increasing sequences of functions, with the appropriate adjustments.
