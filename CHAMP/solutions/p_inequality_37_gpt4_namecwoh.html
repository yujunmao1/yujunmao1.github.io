The inequality of arithmetic and geometric means (AM-GM inequality) is a fundamental principle in mathematical analysis and optimization. It states that for a set of non-negative numbers, the arithmetic mean is always greater than or equal to the geometric mean. When all the numbers in the set are equal, the arithmetic mean and the geometric mean are equal.

Let's consider a set of n non-negative real numbers a1, a2, ..., an. The arithmetic mean (AM) of these numbers is calculated as the sum of the numbers divided by the number of elements, i.e., (a1 + a2 + ... + an) / n. The geometric mean (GM) is calculated as the nth root of the product of the numbers, i.e., (a1 * a2 * ... * an)^(1/n).

The AM-GM inequality states that AM ≥ GM. In other words, (a1 + a2 + ... + an) / n ≥ (a1 * a2 * ... * an)^(1/n).

This inequality is widely used in various fields of mathematics, including algebra, calculus, optimization, and number theory. It is also used in physics and engineering to derive certain inequalities and bounds. The AM-GM inequality is a special case of the more general Hölder's inequality and can be proven using several methods, including mathematical induction, convexity of functions, and the rearrangement inequality.
