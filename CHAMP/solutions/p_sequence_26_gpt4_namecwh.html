The Monotone Convergence Theorem is a fundamental theorem in analysis, a branch of mathematics, that deals with sequences of real numbers. It is also known as Lebesgue's Monotone Convergence Theorem in the context of Lebesgue integration.

The theorem states that if a sequence of real numbers is monotone (meaning it is either entirely non-increasing or non-decreasing) and bounded, then the sequence is convergent. In simpler terms, if you have a sequence of numbers that is always going up or always going down, and it doesn't go to infinity or negative infinity, then the sequence will approach a specific number.

In the context of Lebesgue integration, the theorem is used to assert that the limit of the integral of a sequence of functions is equal to the integral of the limit of the functions, provided the functions in the sequence are non-negative and each function in the sequence is less than or equal to the next function.

This theorem is a key tool in mathematical analysis and probability theory, as it allows us to interchange limits and integrals, which is a common step in many proofs and computations.
