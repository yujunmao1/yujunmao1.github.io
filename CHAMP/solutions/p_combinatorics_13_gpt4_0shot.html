Of 3n+1 objects, n are indistinguishable, and the remaining ones are distinct. In how many ways can we choose n objects, as an expression of n?
This problem involves the concept of combinations in mathematics. 

The total number of objects is 3n+1. Out of these, n are indistinguishable, and the remaining (3n+1-n) = 2n+1 are distinct.

When we choose n objects, there are two cases to consider:

1. We choose some or all of the indistinguishable objects.
2. We do not choose any of the indistinguishable objects.

Case 1: We choose some or all of the indistinguishable objects.

Since all the indistinguishable objects are the same, we don't have to consider the different ways of choosing them. We just need to consider the number of distinct objects we choose. This can range from 0 to n (since we can't choose more than n objects in total). 

So, the number of ways to choose the distinct objects is the sum of the combinations of choosing r distinct objects, where r ranges from 0 to n. This is given by the formula:

Sum_{r=0}^{n} C(2n+1, r)

Case 2: We do not choose any of the indistinguishable objects.

In this case, we have to choose all n objects from the distinct ones. The number of ways to do this is given by the combination formula:

C(2n+1, n)

So, the total number of ways to choose n objects is the sum of the number of ways in the two cases:

Sum_{r=0}^{n} C(2n+1, r) + C(2n+1, n)
