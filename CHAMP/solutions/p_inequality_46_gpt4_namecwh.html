The Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality is a fundamental theorem in the field of mathematics, specifically in linear algebra and analysis. It provides a relationship between the dot product of two vectors and the magnitude of each vector.

The inequality is named after Augustin-Louis Cauchy and Hermann Amandus Schwarz, who independently proved it.

The inequality states that for all vectors of real or complex numbers, the absolute value of the dot product of the vectors is less than or equal to the product of the magnitudes (or lengths) of the two vectors.

Mathematically, if we have two vectors u and v, the Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality can be written as:

|u•v| ≤ ||u|| ||v||

where:
- u•v is the dot product of u and v,
- ||u|| is the magnitude (or length) of vector u,
- ||v|| is the magnitude (or length) of vector v.

The equality holds if and only if the two vectors are linearly dependent, i.e., one vector is a scalar multiple of the other.

The Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality has many important applications in mathematics, including proofs of other theorems and inequalities, and in the fields of physics and engineering.
