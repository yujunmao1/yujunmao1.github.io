The rearrangement inequality is a fundamental theorem in mathematics that deals with the sum of products in sequences. It states that for any two sequences of real numbers, the sum of the products of corresponding terms is at least as great as the sum of the products of any other arrangement of terms.

If these sequences are arranged in non-decreasing order, i.e., a1 ≤ a2 ≤ ... ≤ an and b1 ≤ b2 ≤ ... ≤ bn, then for any permutation σ of the numbers {1, 2, ..., n}, the following inequality holds:

a1*b1 + a2*b2 + ... + an*bn ≥ a1*bσ(1) + a2*bσ(2) + ... + an*bσ(n)

This means that the sum of the products of corresponding terms in the original sequences is greater than or equal to the sum of the products of the terms in any other arrangement (permutation) of the sequences.
