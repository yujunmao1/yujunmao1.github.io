Chebyshev's Inequality is a fundamental theorem in probability theory and statistics which provides a general bound on the probability that a random variable deviates from its mean. It is named after the Russian mathematician Pafnuty Chebyshev.

The inequality states that no more than 1/k^2 of the distribution's values can be more than k standard deviations away from the mean (or equivalently, at least 1-1/k^2 of the distribution's values are within k standard deviations of the mean). Here, k is any positive real number greater than one.

Mathematically, it can be expressed as:

P(|X - μ| ≥ kσ) ≤ 1/k²

where:
- P is the probability,
- X is a random variable,
- μ is the mean (expected value) of the random variable X,
- σ is the standard deviation of X,
- k is the number of standard deviations bounding the range.

This inequality holds true for any probability distribution with defined variance, including those without a defined mean. It's a very useful inequality because it can be applied to any distribution, unlike the similar rules of thumb such as the empirical rule which only applies to normal distributions.
